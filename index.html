Who's Backing Who? Where Queens Borough President Candidates Are Getting Their Campaign Contributions

By Joseph Jungermann

Before the COVID-19 pandemic hit the United States and New York hard, New York City was set to hold a special election on March 24 to fill the vacant Queens Borough President spot formerly held by current Queens D.A. Melinda Katz.

With no word yet on when the election will be rescheduled, remaining candidates sit on a bed of campaign finance contributions that have helped lift their candidacies to this point.

But not all candidate's bank accounts were created equally.

Despite front runners like current City Council members Donovan Richards (D-Queens) and Costa Constantinides (D-Queens) and former City Council member Elizabeth Crowley raising hundreds of thousands of dollars, all three have gotten a boost through other contributor types that are not listed as individuals, according to the New York City Campaign Finance Board.

Below is a financial breakdown for the remaining six candidates.

Individual contributions do make up more than half of each candidate's total funds received, but other avenues have given the top three earners an edge.

But perhaps no candidate has received such a clear boost from a specific source than Crowley. According to the financial data, the former Council Member has a clear edge over her competitors when it comes to donations received from labor unions, raising $8,450 in collective funds from that group--$7,000 more than her nearest competitor, former Queens Assistant District Attorney James Quinn.

Crowley's labor union donations only account for 2% of her total contributions received, but it's the attention of this group that matters the most. Queens has a large working-class population where the median household income sits at just under $65K and the civilian labor force is 64.1%, according to the U.S. Census Bureau.

With a strong list of labor unions representing firefighters, steelworkers, teachers, first responders and the like, Crowley could have some extra support from the city's working class population.

Considering that this group has been hit the hardest during the coronavirus pandemic, Crowley could experience an influx in voter turnout from middle-class workers who are looking for a favorable candidate and direct line to the mayor's office, which will be dealing with economic shortfalls for years to come.
